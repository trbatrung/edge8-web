3. Why Shopify can't anchor the Vietnam core

This is not a preference, it is a structural limit. Shopify Payments is not available to a Vietnam-registered business, and a headless storefront still hands the shopper to Shopify's hosted checkout to take payment, which is governed by the country of the store's business entity. A Vietnam-entity store is therefore pushed onto local gateways anyway, which means you are integrating Vietnamese payments directly whether or not Shopify is in the picture. If you are doing that work regardless, Shopify's main reason to exist for this market falls away.

Since you integrate these rails directly, here is the provider landscape, in plain terms. No single Vietnamese provider gives you every method on its own: pick one main gateway for cards, QR, and bank transfer, then add the one or two e-wallets your customers actually reach for.

The one-line takeaway. Payoo is a strong, properly licensed gateway covering cards, QR, bank transfer, and instalments through a single integration. What it does not hand you is MoMo and ZaloPay, Vietnam's two dominant e-wallets, which are separate integrations. Plan a main gateway plus those wallets as add-ons.

Payoo

Customer can pay with: domestic bank cards (40+ Vietnamese banks), Visa and Mastercard, VietQR / QR code, bank transfer, 0% instalments, and pay-at-store. Role: a broad, single-integration gateway. Does not include MoMo or ZaloPay.

VNPAY Strong default

Customer can pay with: VNPAY-QR (scannable inside 40+ banking apps), domestic and international cards, and bank transfer. Role: bank-backed and very widely used — a sensible primary gateway for the Vietnam store.

MoMo Add-on

Customer can pay with: the MoMo wallet (Vietnam's #1, tens of millions of users), MoMo QR, and linked cards or bank accounts. Role: a separate integration on top of your main gateway. Leaving it out costs real conversions.

ZaloPay Add-on

Customer can pay with: the ZaloPay wallet, ZaloPay QR, and linked bank or card. Role: tied to Zalo, Vietnam's main messaging app, so reach is large among younger buyers. Its own integration, like MoMo.

2C2P

Customer can pay with: international and domestic cards, QR, local wallets, bank transfer, and instalments, across Vietnam and the wider region. Role: a regional gateway worth a look if you later want one provider that stretches beyond Vietnam.

VietQR & cash on delivery

What they are: VietQR is the national bank-transfer QR standard nearly every banking app reads, and most gateways above can present it. Cash on delivery is still common and builds trust on a first order. Keep both available.

Provider capabilities and fees change often, and Payoo does not publish a full method-and-fee list. Confirm the exact card networks, the precise wallet list, settlement currency, and pricing directly with each provider before committing.

This is the strategic heart of the proposal, and it is the strongest reason to go custom. In the AI era, your data is your moat. A custom build means every order, customer, and interaction lives in a database Arca owns outright: queryable, exportable, and ready to feed AI models, personalisation, and analytics, with no platform sitting in the middle. Shopify, by contrast, keeps your customer and order data inside its system. You can export it, but the platform stands between you and your own data, and the richest behavioural signal stays on their side of the line.

On adoption, Arca owns everything. The full codebase and the customer database transfer to Arca outright. No license, no lock-in, no per-seat platform tax on your own records. The asset you are building is not just a store, it is a clean, owned data foundation that compounds in value as you layer AI on top of it.

One practical note that comes with owning data: once you hold customer records, you are responsible for them under Vietnam's PDPD (Decree 13), and under Australia's Privacy Act, Canada's PIPEDA, and US state laws such as CCPA if you later sell into those markets. None of this is a blocker. We build consent at collection and a deletion flow in from day one rather than retrofitting it.

7. The open question: fulfilment

At zero to 50 orders a day, fulfilment is an operations choice, not a build blocker. It is the one decision still genuinely open, so here are the options and our recommended starting point.

Start in-house, one carrier Recommended for v1

Pack orders yourself and integrate a single Vietnamese carrier (GHN, GHTK, ViettelPost, J&T, or Ninja Van) into the build: create a shipment, pull tracking, write it to your database, and email the customer automatically. Cheapest, and comfortable well past 50 a day.

3PL pick-pack-ship Later

Outsource warehousing and fulfilment to a Vietnamese 3PL when packing time becomes the bottleneck. Real, but premature at your current volume and avoidable cost early on.

Same-day / local courier

For Ho Chi Minh City and Hanoi, an on-demand courier (Ahamove, Grab) can be added for fast local delivery. Optional, slots into the same order flow.

How it plugs in

Whatever you choose, fulfilment is a phase-2 integration against the same commerce core. It does not gate the storefront, payments, or launch — you can go live and add carrier automation right after.

Our recommendation: launch with in-house fulfilment plus one carrier API, and add a 3PL only when volume or packing time justifies it.
