# Chapter 1: Two questions

In the spring of 2026 I found out I was an imposter.

There was no board meeting and no investor with a spreadsheet. It took two questions, asked in the same season by two people who were not trying to expose anyone. One came from a student in a class I was teaching. The other came from a prospect on a sales call. Ordinary questions, asked in good faith, and I could not answer either one the way I answer things I am proud of.

Some context, so the questions land on you the way they landed on me. I run an AI transformation firm. Through the AI Officer Institute, I had been teaching founders and CEOs that the job of leadership had doubled. Half of it is the job you know: hire well, build a culture, set direction, hold people accountable. The other half arrived quietly over the last few years: directing AI, not just managing the people who use it. I taught that this second half rests on three skills you cannot buy: designing workflows where some of the workers are agents, organizing information so AI can actually use it (we call the discipline ABC, Always Be Cataloguing), and writing instructions precise enough that a machine builds the thing you pictured. And I taught that underneath everything sits one unglamorous thing, a central database, one place where anyone in the company, a person or an AI agent, goes to know what is true.

I believed all of it. I still do. That was never the problem.

The first question came in class. Someone asked how many AI agents I had running in my own company.

It is my own question. It is the diagnostic I teach: look at your org chart and count the agents on each team. I had aimed it at other people's companies more times than I could count. Aimed back at mine, it stopped being a teaching question. The honest answer was not a number I wanted to say out loud in a room full of people who were paying to learn this from me.

The second question came on a sales call. The man asked to see a demo of what a company database looks like. Not a description. A demo. Show, not tell.

The closest thing I had was built for someone else.

A few months earlier I had centralized the database for EO Vietnam, a business group I belong to. That work was real and it held up. It also proved something I did not want to look at, which is that I was capable of building the thing I taught, and had built it, for everyone except us. When I reached for Edge8's own database, the thing I would have put in front of that prospect, there was no demo. There was not even a database, not in the sense I used the word when I taught. Our data was in so many different places that the count itself was the embarrassment.

And it was not the only exhibit. My leadership coach, an AI tool of our own, was half built too. Some of it mine, some of it my team's, none of it finished.

Two questions in one season, pointing the same direction. The floor I taught as the foundation of everything was, at my own company, a scatter. The tool that carried my name was half done. I had a private sentence I had been afraid of for a while, and that spring it stopped being hypothetical: I was becoming someone who teaches but cannot do. I have always hated that person. I was on my way to being him.

There were reasonable ways out. I could have assigned it. I run a company with engineers in it. I could have written a brief, asked for a working system by the end of the quarter, and reviewed the result in a meeting. It would have looked like leadership. It would have been procurement, the exact thing I stood up and told other founders not to do. I knew the standard escape routes because I warned people about them for a living. Buy tools, and you get a drawer of logins. Buy a course, and you get certificates. Rent a consultant, and the judgment leaves when they do. I had said versions of that in public too many times, which closed every door except the honest one.

Instead I did the work myself.

I opened Claude and told it what I wanted to do and how I wanted the data structures to look. For reference, I pointed it at the places our data actually lived. Then we went back and forth on the architecture until I felt good about it. I did not type the code. I directed, and the AI wrote, which is the third of the three skills I had been teaching and, until then, only half practicing on my own business.

Once the architecture held, I started moving data, one office at a time, through the same lens I teach: Revenue, Operations, Talent, Innovation. I used engineers to check and extend what I built, not to start from scratch.

None of this was impressive to watch. There is no montage in cataloguing. The work was deciding what was true, writing it down, and putting it where a person or an agent could find it in seconds instead of asking around. I teach a blunt rule: if your AI cannot find it, it does not exist. By that rule, an embarrassing amount of my own company did not exist.

Everything in that build was something I had taught. Deciding the flow of the work. Organizing the information. Writing instructions precise enough that the result was the thing I pictured. Teaching those skills is not the same thing as having them. They are built the way judgment is built, by doing the real work on your own business, and I had been outsourcing exactly that to my own reputation.

Here is what happened next, and I want to be careful with it, because the easy version of this story is a lie.

Edge8 is about twenty percent smaller in 2026 than it was. That is mostly attrition. People left on their own, and I chose not to backfill every seat one for one. I let two people go. I am not proud of that, and I will not dress it up as strategy. Over the same window, revenue is up about sixty percent since January. As people left, AI absorbed the work, the structure held, and the business grew anyway. Revenue per person roughly doubled.

That gap, a fifth fewer people and far more revenue, is what I mean when I say leverage is a number and not a vibe. It is also why I trust the rest of what is in this book. Not because I read about it. Because I failed my own test first, and then took it again.

I am telling you this before anything else because I think you may be where I was, just without the bad luck of getting asked in public. You have spent on AI. Seats, subscriptions, maybe a pilot, maybe a working group with a deck. The P&L has not moved, and some quiet part of you has started to wonder whether the failure is the tools or you.

So let me pass along the two questions, exactly as they found me. How many AI agents are running in your company right now? Not licensed. Running. And if someone asked you tomorrow to see, not hear about, the one place your company goes to know what is true, what would you show them?

If the honest answers are none and nothing, you are not behind on tools. You are where I was in the spring of 2026, staffed and structured for half of a job that has doubled, and the other half does not care how good you are at the first one.

This book is what I did about my own answers, and what it cost.
