# Chapter 5: The question that lands like a slap

The second-Thursday group met in the back room at Antonelli's, fourteen owners around one long table, and had for as long as anyone could document. The rules were old and simple. What was said in the room stayed in the room. Nobody sold anybody anything. And every January, one sheet of paper each, real numbers, face down until the last man was seated.

AI came up before the bread did. It came up every month now, the way the weather comes up, and with about as much control over it.

Gil Ferran, who did commercial paving, had spent sixty thousand dollars on a customer service tool that, in his words, lied to his customers politely and around the clock. A man who ran cold storage said his consultant's roadmap had a roadmap. Somebody's nephew had automated something and broken payroll. The laughter went around the table like a basket of rolls, and underneath the laughter, unspoken, was the thing the room actually met for, which was relief. Everyone had spent. Nobody had numbers. Therefore the tools were not ready, and no one at this table was a fool.

Marcus told the board story. He had it polished to a shine by now: Ellery's folder, the one-page P&L, point to the line. He gave them her question word for word, the tools or the leadership, and paused where the pause went, and the table groaned on cue.

"Boards read airline magazines," said the cold storage man. "That's the whole disease."

"One quarter," Gil said, shaking his head. "To show a return on software. I've got paving jobs that cure slower than that."

The sympathy was a warm bath, and Marcus sat in it, and it did not quite reach the cold.

Priya Okonkwo-Reyes sat where she always sat, near the end, mostly listening. She ran custom metal fabrication two towns from Halden and had been in the group eleven years. She was not a quiet woman, exactly. She spent her words the way estimators spend contingency, only where something might actually move. In January her sheet had stopped the room for a full minute, revenue per head up by something like half in two years, and when they had pressed her on it she had said, "We had a good year," in the voice of a woman reporting rain, and passed the basket.

Tonight the needle came around to her on schedule.

"Ask Priya," Gil said. "Priya, tell Marcus which robot to buy."

"I don't have a robot to recommend," Priya said, and let the conversation go on past her, and it did.

Marcus caught up with her in the parking lot. She was at her truck, keys already out, and he came at her the way he came at bankers, warm, direct, a little too sure of the welcome.

"You didn't say much in there."

"There wasn't much being said."

"I wanted to ask you something. The board thing. You must get the same questions, your board, your bank, whoever keeps you up at night." He put his hands in his pockets. "How do you answer them?"

Priya looked at him for a moment. In the sodium light of the lot she looked mostly tired, the way a person looks at the end of a day that started at five, and later he would understand that the tiredness was the most honest credential she showed him.

"Can I ask you one first?"

"Go ahead."

"How much of your week goes to leading the AI? Not buying it. Leading it."

"I have a director who..."

"Not who leads it for you," she said. "You. Hours, in your week. Name them."

Marcus opened his mouth. He was a man who could account for every dollar in his building, who knew his freight costs to the point and his scrap rate to the decimal, and he stood in a restaurant parking lot with his week laid out in front of him and could not put his finger on one hour. There was the operations review, where Trevor gave updates. There was the demo in January. There was the signing of things.

"I'd have to think about it," he said.

"That's the answer," Priya said. It was not cruel. It was the way a doctor says a number out loud while reading the chart. "It was my answer too, for about two years. The two years cost more than the tools did."

She opened the truck door.

"Good luck with your board, Marcus."

He drove home the long way, through town, doing arithmetic he had never once thought to do. Monday: floor, bank call, ops review. Tuesday. He went through the week the way he had gone through Grace's box, front to back, looking for the thing itself, and the week was full, the week was always full, he had been proud his whole life of how full the week was. Somewhere in it there was an hour for the roof warranty. There was an hour for the sign by the interstate, twice a year. There was no hour, anywhere, in any week going back eighteen months, for the four hundred thousand dollars that was supposed to change the company.

Ellery had asked him to point at a line. Priya had asked him to point at an hour. The money and the time, and he could not put his finger on either one.

The long red at Route 9 caught him a mile from home. He sat through the whole of it counting, and the light turned green before he found a single hour, and the car behind him had to honk him forward.
