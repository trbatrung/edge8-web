# Chapter 6: Come watch a Tuesday

The brochure was on his desk by Wednesday, because Grace was efficient even in the service of a bad idea.

DIRECTING AI: A STRATEGIC INTENSIVE FOR SENIOR EXECUTIVES. Four days in Scottsdale. A faculty of practitioners. A capstone simulation. Fourteen thousand dollars, not counting the room, and a photograph of men his age in open collars looking capably at a screen.

He read it twice, and caught himself nodding, and the nodding was what scared him. He knew the shape of this decision. It was the shape of every decision in Grace's box downstairs: a check, written at the problem, from a safe distance. He put the brochure face down, which was becoming a habit with documents, and asked Grace to get Mai Bennett on the phone.

She came on at six, with shop noise dying behind her.

"Marcus."

"I'll ask you one question and let you get home. The thing you built over there. Who did you hire to build it? Give me the name, the firm, whatever it was, and I'll leave you alone."

There was a pause on the line, and in the pause he could hear her deciding something.

"Nobody," she said.

"Mai."

"Nobody, Marcus. I built the first of it myself. Nights, at my kitchen table, for the better part of a year, and I was bad at it for months. Then my people built the rest, on their own jobs, because they'd watched me be bad at it and live."

"You have a company to run."

"I ran it the whole time." No heat in it. "You're asking the wrong species of question. You're asking who, so you can pay them. There was no who. That was the whole trick."

He tried the other doors, one at a time, and she closed them without slamming any.

"Then tell me what you'd charge to come talk to my team. One afternoon. Name any number."

"Nothing, because I won't do it. If I explain it in your conference room, it turns into a deck. You have decks." A forklift beeped somewhere behind her. "You'd frame the good parts and change nothing. I've watched it happen. I've been the deck."

"Then what," Marcus said, and heard his own voice fray, "am I supposed to do? The board gave me a quarter. I'm three weeks into it. Everyone I could hire, you're telling me not to hire. Everything I could buy, you're telling me not to buy. What's left?"

The line was quiet a moment.

"Come watch a Tuesday," Mai said.

"What?"

"Come to my shop and watch a Tuesday. All of it. Not a tour, not a presentation. A regular day, from the morning meeting on."

"What happens on Tuesday?"

"Nothing," she said. "That's the point. Anybody can put on a show for a visitor for an hour. What you need to see is nothing special happening, all day, the way it happens now. The show is that there's no show."

He turned the brochure over, looked at the men in the open collars, turned it back down.

"One condition," she said. "You come to watch, not to shop. Ask me what anything costs, ask me the name of one product, and the day's over and we'll still be friends. Agreed?"

"Agreed."

"Tuesday. Seven o'clock, the side door by the ramp. If you come at nine you'll miss it."

"Miss what?"

"That's what everybody asks," Mai said, and for the first time in the call she sounded almost amused. "Seven o'clock, Marcus."

She hung up the way shop people hang up, without ceremony.

Marcus sat for a while with his hand still on the receiver. Then he buzzed Grace, and dropped the brochure in the wastebasket where she would see it and know the Scottsdale question was settled.

"Clear next Tuesday," he said. "The whole day."

"The whole day." Grace's pen was ready. "What do I tell people it is?"

He thought about it.

"Tell them I'm at a shop two towns over," he said, "watching somebody work."

Thursday morning Steve appeared in his doorway with a legal pad of his own.

"Grace mentioned Tuesday," he said. "The fabricator visit. I think it's a great instinct. Let me build you a brief. Their probable stack, an evaluation rubric, the questions worth asking. And I'm glad to drive out with you and take the notes, so you can stay at altitude."

It was, Marcus thought, exactly the right offer. It was thorough and professional and generous, and it was the whole eighteen months standing in one doorway: somebody else would go learn it, and Marcus would stay at altitude.

"No brief," Marcus said. "No rubric. And it's just me going."

Steve blinked. "Can I ask what the objective is?"

Marcus looked at the legal pad in the doorway, and then at his own on the desk, where the login card had sat since the night he finally used the password, square in the middle, waiting for him to decide what it meant.

"I don't know yet," he said. "That's the objective."

He heard how it sounded. Steve, to his credit, wrote nothing down. He stood there another moment, a man listening for instructions in a language he had been hired to translate, and then he went back down the hall, and Marcus turned the card over once on the pad and left it where it lay.
