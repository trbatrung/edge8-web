# Chapter 26: The hardest conversation

The floor shut down at six. Marcus waited on the mezzanine until the last of the finishing crew had crossed the lot, then came down the open stairs and walked the length of his own dark floor to the sales desk, where one lamp was burning, because one lamp was always burning.

Ray Delgado sat under it with a pencil in his hand, a binder open in front of him, the old ten-key at his elbow with its curl of paper tape. The good chair, the leather one the customers sat in, stood empty on the near side of the desk.

"If you wanted the good chair, you should have come at five," Ray said, not looking up.

Marcus pulled the metal stool over from the sample rack and sat on that.

The pencil moved a while longer. Then it stopped. Ray laid it in the spine of the binder, took off his glasses, folded them, and looked at Marcus the way he looked at a set of drawings that would not add up.

"You've got your bank voice sitting in your throat," he said. "Don't use it on me."

"All right."

"Say it plain."

"I'm here to let you go, Ray."

Under the floor the compressor kicked on, ran its cycle, and quit.

"Well," Ray said.

He looked at the open binder for a moment. Then he closed it, carefully, as if the binder were the thing that needed gentle handling.

"Is this Brandt?"

"No."

"We lost Brandt to a shop that quoted it in a day, and their number was real. I checked it. Jobs that size don't get won on a fake number." He said it evenly, the estimator in him conceding the point because it was true. "So it's a fair question."

"It isn't Brandt. It's me."

Ray waited.

"Two months ago I brought you the quoting work and you said no, and I let it go. I stood up in front of this company and said we were naming every outcome in the building, and then I carved out the only one that mattered, because it was yours. I told myself that was loyalty." Marcus kept his eyes on him. "It wasn't loyalty to you. It was loyalty to me. You quoting by hand is the last corner of this place that still runs the way I built it. As long as your desk didn't change, I didn't have to feel what changing costs. I made you my excuse, Ray. And the bar moved. It moved for everybody on that floor. Dana had to move. Sam. The two kids in purchasing. I held it still for you, and every person who moved has been paying the difference. You'd have wanted that truth then. You're getting it now. That part is mine, and I'm sorry for it."

Ray turned the folded glasses over in his fingers.

"You should have said it then."

"I know."

"I'd have argued. But you should have said it."

For a while neither of them spoke.

"The chance was real," Marcus said. "Sam offered you an hour every morning for as long as it took. Dana cleared her calendar twice."

"I know it was real." Ray set the glasses down on the binder. "And I was never going to take it, and we both knew that the day you asked. We just let each other pretend. That was a kindness, and kindness is the wrong tool for some jobs. The kind answer was the honest one, early. What I got was the kind silence, late."

The paper tape on the ten-key stirred in the draft from the dock doors and went still.

"You want to know why," Ray said. It was not a question.

"You told me. A machine doesn't know the customer."

"That's what I said. It's not the whole of it." He looked out past the lamp at the dark floor. "I've been pricing work for thirty years, Marcus. Since before this building. I don't do it at this desk. I do it walking a floor, or standing in some man's unfinished lobby while he tells me what he's scared of. The number comes up in me like a temperature. Then I come back here and run the arithmetic to prove what I already know." He almost smiled. "Everybody thinks the eleven days is arithmetic. The arithmetic takes an afternoon. The rest is me carrying the job around until I'm sure. Because when I hand a man a number, I'm not handing him a number. I'm handing him my word."

"I know."

"Dana wrote down what she knew and there was a bigger job standing on the other side of the paper. I watched it happen. I'm glad for her, and I mean that." He tapped the closed binder once. "There's no bigger job on the other side of mine. The feel is the job. Take the feel out, and what's left is a man who used to."

The compressor ran again. Neither of them moved until it finished.

"Eighteen months, full salary," Marcus said. "Health coverage for you and Marion until you're sixty-five. The truck stays yours. The announcement gets written by you, whatever words you want in it, or no words at all."

"That's too rich."

"It's short of what I owe you, and I'm not negotiating."

Ray let it stand. Then he looked at the lamp for a moment.

"Thirty years I priced everything that came through that door," he said. "Turns out I was the one job you couldn't quote straight."

Marcus did not answer that. There was no answer that deserved to be said out loud.

"And I'm asking for something," Marcus said finally. "I've got no right to ask it, so you can send me up those stairs empty."

"You want what's in my head."

Marcus looked at the desk pad under Ray's forearms, gone gray with graphite, thirty years of phone numbers written over each other in a small careful hand. The first catalogue Halden had ever had, and the only copy.

"What you'll give. A month, with Sam. Walk him through the jobs. The customers. What you look for in a lobby. Why you passed on the Fenwick job when the margin looked fine. It goes into the catalogue, next to everybody else's. Not so a machine can be you, Ray. It'll be faster and worse. Then faster and good enough. It won't ever be you. So the people who stay don't pay for my mistake twice."

Ray was quiet a long time. Then he opened the binder again and turned it around on the desk so it faced Marcus.

"Ashford," he said. "Day six." Columns of figures in pencil, neat as type, and down the right edge a narrow column with no header, four short notes in it. He put his finger on the first one. "Their architect redlines everything twice, so you don't order veneer off his first drawing, ever. Their dock won't take a fifty-three-foot trailer, which nobody thinks to say until there's a driver standing in it. And Kessler's mill has been slipping ship dates since the son took over, so anything Kessler touches gets a week it doesn't deserve." He sat back. "None of that is in a price book. There are no formulas, Marcus. There's weather."

Marcus took the fat black pen out of his shirt pocket, slid a blank sheet off the stack in Ray's tray, and wrote one word at the top of it.

Weather.

Ray watched the pen move and said nothing for a moment.

"You know what it is, what you're asking. A month of that, and when you're done, the drawers are still full, and everything in them is somewhere else."

"Yes," Marcus said. "That's exactly what it is."

"Sam," Ray said. "Not a consultant. Nobody with a deck."

"Sam."

"He asks decent questions, for a kid who thinks a spreadsheet is a personality." The dry came back into his voice for one line, and left again. "A month. What I'll give is what I can find words for. Going in, you should know the best of it doesn't have any."

"I know that too," Marcus said. "One more thing. The front room. Where the customers come. The samples, the coffee that's never been any good. Your name is going over the door. Delgado. It goes up whether you like it or not, so save the argument."

Ray looked at him.

"People will think I died."

"They'll think you built the place."

"I built some of it." He picked the pencil up, turned it over, set it down. "No picture. Just the name."

"Just the name."

Marcus stood up off the stool. His knees were stiff and he let them be stiff.

"When do you want me gone?" Ray asked.

"End of the month. And the door doesn't lock behind you. I mean that."

"It always locks, Marcus." There was no bitterness in it. It was an estimator's read, delivered like all the others. "That's all right. It's supposed to."

Marcus stood there a moment longer, because there should have been something else to say, and there wasn't.

Ray put his glasses back on and pulled the binder around to his side of the desk.

"I'd like to land Ashford before I go," he said. "Nobody else knows they want the freight broken out by floor."

"Take whatever it needs."

"It needs five more days." He squared the pages with his thumb, the way Marcus squared his own. "Some jobs take eleven, Marcus. That part I never once got wrong."

Marcus crossed the dark floor to the stairs. At the bottom he stopped, one hand on the steel rail, and looked back at the lamp. One light, one bent head, the second name ever printed on a Halden timecard, working.

He was halfway up when the ten-key started, small and far off and steady, the sound of a man proving what he already knew.
