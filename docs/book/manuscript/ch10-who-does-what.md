# Chapter 10: Who does what

He was halfway to the truck when he turned around and came back up her stairs, and Mai looked up from her desk as if she had been timing him.

"One more question. The real one."

"Go."

"An afternoon instead of eleven days." Marcus tapped the pad. "That's the whole case, isn't it. That's what I put in front of my board. Speed."

"No." She came around the desk to the whiteboard, where the four boxes still stood. "Speed is what you can see from the parking lot. If speed were the thing, you could buy it, and you'd be right back where you started, with a fast version of nothing." She picked up the marker but didn't write, just pointed with it, box to box. "The thing is that I can tell you who does what, and why. Every step. Ask me."

"The intake. Why an agent?"

"Because reading a four-hundred-page request is not judgment, it's checking four hundred facts against a catalogue, and machines don't skim when they're tired. A person doing intake is a person wasted."

"The takeoff."

"Same answer. Arithmetic against known facts. The agent does in a night what a person does in four days, and it does it without pride, which matters, because it will flag forty things a proud man would guess at."

"And Teresa."

"Judgment. Which flags matter. What the customer is actually afraid of. Whether we want the job. Where the margin should sit on a one-off. No agent rules on those, not because it can't produce an answer, but because the answer is a promise, and I will not let a machine make promises with my company's name on them." She capped the marker. "Who does what, Marcus. That's the whole design. You've been doing it your entire life, with crews. The only thing that changed is that some of the who aren't people now. They don't get tired, they don't get proud, and they can't be trusted to guess."

"You said that before. That they find something anyway."

"The bad ones guess politely," Mai said. "Politely is the dangerous part. A man who's guessing sounds like he's guessing. An agent that's guessing sounds like a printed page." She looked at him. "Which is why every agent in this building has one rule burned in above every other rule, and a person whose name goes on the output. You design the who. You don't skip the who."

Marcus looked at the four boxes a while.

"It's a floor layout," he said slowly. "That's all this is. It's the first year in the building and I'm deciding where the saws go and which bench feeds which bench and who's standing at each one. I've done this."

"You've done this," Mai agreed. "Twenty-two years of it. Nobody at that bootcamp told you that, did they. That the second half of the job would turn out to be the first skill you ever had."

She walked him down herself this time. At the door to the lot she stopped.

"Homework, since you're the type. Draw your own quote the way I drew mine. Not the way it should be. The way it is, today, every real step. You'll hate it. Draw it anyway."

He sat in her parking lot with the legal pad against the wheel and did it before he could soften, because he knew himself, and by tonight the honest version would have sanded itself smooth.

It took fourteen boxes.

The request came in to Ron, or to Casey if Ron was walking a site, and sat until Ron read it. Drawings went out to the floor for a look and came back when they came back. Suppliers got called, and called back, or didn't, and got called again. The folder went to the controller for rates, which meant it waited for Thursday, because rates were a Thursday thing. Somewhere in the second week it came upstairs for his signature and sat in his own inbox, and he wrote HOLD, or FINE, or SHARPEN THIS, and it went back down. Fourteen boxes, and when he was finished he made himself count the ones where work was actually happening, pencil moving, mind engaged, and the ones where the job was simply standing in line, waiting for a particular head to be free.

Eleven of the fourteen were waiting.

Most of the eleven days, drawn honestly, was the job standing in line outside one man's head. The head was brilliant. The line was the business.

He sat in her lot until he had it all down, boxes and arrows and the waiting drawn as waiting. It was ugly, and it was his, and drawing it had taken forty minutes he would once have spent on the phone.

Mai's question from the parking lot of Antonelli's came back to him, the one he had failed in front of her. How much of your week goes to leading the AI. Not buying it. Leading it.

He looked at the pad against the wheel.

An hour last Thursday. Three hours with the whiteboard today. Forty minutes in a gravel lot drawing his own company the way it actually was.

He could name the hours now. There weren't many yet. But they had started, and he knew, starting the truck, that they were the first hours in eighteen months that had cost him nothing and were worth anything.
