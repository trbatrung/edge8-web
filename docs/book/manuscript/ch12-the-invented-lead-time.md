# Chapter 12: The invented lead time

The near miss came the following Wednesday, and it came, because the universe has a sense of humor, on a Brandt job.

It was small, as Brandt work went. A punch-list add-on for the lobby out by the airport: reception millwork, rift white oak, eight pieces. Ron was walking a site two hours south, and Casey, trying to be useful, had done what the whole building was quietly starting to do that month. He had asked the machine.

The confirmation the agent drafted was clean. Quantities right, finishes right, freight right, and in the middle of it, stated the way printed things state things: Lead time, fourteen days.

Casey had it addressed and ready for the afternoon send when Dan Mercer passed by on his way to the dock, read it upside down out of pure habit, and put one finger on it like he was pinning a moth.

"Where did fourteen days come from?"

Casey didn't know. The draft knew, or seemed to. Fourteen days sat there in the same font as all the true things around it, confident, symmetrical, the kind of number that gets promised.

"Kessler's been running eight weeks since the son took over," Dan said. "Everybody knows that."

Marcus, who had come down for something else entirely, stood at the edge of it and watched the arithmetic no one had to do out loud. Fourteen days promised, fifty-six delivered. Brandt's crew standing in a finished lobby waiting six weeks for eight pieces of white oak, on the biggest year of Brandt's life, with Brandt's name on the front of the building and Halden's name on the delay.

"It didn't go out," Casey said, whiter than the oak. "It almost went out."

"It almost went out," Dan agreed, and fixed it in ninety seconds, crossing out the number, writing the true one, initialing the margin. "This is why I check everything." He said it without heat, the way you state a load rating, and went on to the dock, and the whole exchange had cost him two minutes of a day that was full of exchanges exactly like it.

Marcus picked up the corrected draft.

He found the source in a minute of asking. The agent had been given the purchasing spreadsheet in Monday's session, and the spreadsheet had a lead-time column, and the column said fourteen days, because the column had been typed in a year when fourteen days was true and never touched since. The real number, the eight weeks, the son, the slipping, lived in three places: Dan's head, a second spreadsheet Dan kept for himself because he trusted the first one the way you trust a rope you didn't tie, and the margins of Ron's binder two hours south.

The machine had not lied. It had gone where it was allowed to go, found a number wearing a lead time's clothes, and repeated it politely.

The dangerous ones guess politely. She had told him, standing at her whiteboard, and he had written it down without feeling it. Now he felt it. A man who is guessing sounds like he's guessing. Casey would have heard a guess in a colleague's voice, would have said, better check that. Nobody checks a printed page. That was the entire difference between the machine's mistake and the mistakes his company made every single day, filling gaps with confidence, promising off of memory. No difference in kind. Only the speed, and the font.

And Dan had caught it, the way Dan caught everything, and that was the part that followed Marcus back up the stairs and stood behind him at the glass the rest of the afternoon.

Because the catch had cost two minutes and looked like nothing, and it happened, he began to understand, some number of times every day. Dan reading upside down. Dan initialing margins. Dan, the one head that held the true version of Halden Furnish, walking his rounds like a man going through a house at night turning off burners nobody knew were lit.

He thought of Mai's office. Where do your people go when they need to know what's true. They ask Dan. And when Dan's out. They wait.

Except the machine hadn't waited. That was the new fact in the building. The machine did not stand in line outside Dan's head like everyone else. It went around him, at speed, politely, and the only reason eight pieces of white oak hadn't taught Walter Brandt something about Halden that twenty years of lobbies never had was that Dan Mercer happened to walk to the dock the long way.

The hole had been there for years. The machine was just the first employee to fall into it at full speed.

That evening he stood at Dan's office door. He was still there, of course, initialing something.

"The Kessler thing," he said. "Good catch."

"It wasn't a good catch. It was a Tuesday." He looked up. "You want to know how many of those I make a week."

"I'm starting to."

"No," Dan said, not unkindly, turning back to his page. "You're not. Nobody is. That's the job."

Marcus stood in the doorway a moment longer, hearing it. That's the job. He had said those words himself, about other burdens, in other rooms, his whole life, and he had always meant them as a boast.

He was no longer sure how Dan meant them. And it had never once occurred to him, in twenty-two years, to ask what it cost a person, being the only place where the truth was kept.
