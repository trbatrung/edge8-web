# Chapter 13: If your AI can't find it

He called Priya that night from the office, and told her about the fourteen days, and the eight weeks, and the moth pinned to Casey's desk, and Dana turning off burners nobody knew were lit.

She listened all the way through without a sound. When he finished she said, "Good."

"Good?"

"You got your disaster small, early, and free. Most people pay retail." Shop noise banged somewhere behind her and settled. "Now say the lesson back to me. Not what happened. What it means."

"The spreadsheet was wrong."

"Shallower than that. Wronger than that."

Marcus looked at the corrected draft on his desk, Dana's initials in the margin.

"The true number wasn't anywhere," he said slowly. "It was in Dana. The machine can't ask Dana. So as far as the machine is concerned, the truth about Kessler doesn't..." He stopped, because the sentence was arriving somewhere that rearranged the furniture.

"Say it."

"It doesn't exist."

"If your AI can't find it, it doesn't exist," Priya said. "That's the rule. The whole rule. We say it around here the way some shops say safety first. And the second half, the half that makes it a religion instead of a poster: always be cataloguing. Nothing is ever done being written down, because the truth moves. Kessler was fourteen days once. Somebody has to notice when the truth moves and move the writing with it, forever, and that is the least glamorous sentence I will ever say to you, and it is the entire foundation of everything you watched at my shop.

"And when I say written down, I don't mean memos in a shared folder. One place, Marcus. One repository for the whole company. A database for the facts that move, prices, lead times, customers, who decides what. One organized home for the files, the drawings, the standards, the workflows drawn the way you drew yours in my parking lot. If a thing isn't on that floor, it is not true at Halden, however many heads it lives in."

Marcus wrote it on the pad. Both halves.

"And Marcus. Before you hang up and do something proud. What you're feeling right now about Dana."

"I'm not feeling anything about Dana."

"You're feeling that she should have written it down." The line was quiet a moment. "She built you a second spreadsheet because she didn't trust the first one, and she didn't hand it to anybody, and some part of you has already started the sentence that ends with that being her flaw."

He had, in fact, started that sentence. He did not finish it.

"Twenty-two years," Priya said, "who got the raises at Halden? Who got kept in the recession? The ones the building couldn't run without. You paid being needed like it was a skill. Dana learned the lesson you taught, and she learned it better than anybody, and she has been paying your tuition ever since, two minutes at a time, on her way to the dock. The single point of failure isn't her, Marcus. She's the patch. You're the failure."

He sat with that. The compressor cycled somewhere under the floor and quit.

"You have a hard way of putting things."

"I had a Dana," Priya said. "Me. I was my own Dana, which is the only version worse. When we finally wrote the company down, do you know what I found out? Half of what only I knew was wrong. It had been true once and moved, and nobody could check me, because where would they look." A pause. "Go home. Tomorrow, do the boring thing."

The boring thing took him all the next morning, and it was the best morning he had spent in years.

He walked the floor with the legal pad and asked one question at every station, the same question, word for word. If you needed to know something to do this job right, and the person who knows it was out sick, where would you look it up?

The answers came back and every single one of them was a name.

Veneer lead times. Dana. Machine rates. The controller, Thursdays. What Brandt will and won't accept on a finish. Ray. Why the finishing schedule skips the third booth in July. Tomas, and Tomas had to think about who else knew, and came up empty, and looked briefly frightened. The freight rules. Sharon, and everybody pointed at the corner of the whiteboard as if the corner explained itself, and nobody but Sharon could read the corner.

By noon the page was full. He sat in his truck in his own lot and looked at it, the complete catalogue of what Halden Furnish knew, and it was a list of nine names.

It was the answer to the third line on his kitchen-table page, the one he had least wanted answered. Where does the truth of this company live, if not in somebody's head or a drawer of logins. It lived in nine somebodies, and one whiteboard corner that only made sense to a woman named Sharon.

Ellery had asked him, in April, to point at a line. He finally had a document he could point at. Everything the company knew, on one page, and it was a phone directory, and three of the nine were older than sixty, and one of them was a corner of a whiteboard.

He clipped it to the front of the legal pad, where the login card lived. Then he went back inside and stood a while at the edge of his own floor, watching the work move, all of it real, all of it good, all of it running on a library that walked out the door every night at five and drove home in nine different directions.

Always be cataloguing. He had thought, on the phone, that it sounded like drudgery.

Standing there, watching nine libraries walk around his floor in work boots, it sounded like triage.
