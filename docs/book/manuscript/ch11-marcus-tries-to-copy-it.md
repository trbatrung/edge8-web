# Chapter 11: Marcus tries to copy it

Friday morning he commandeered the conference room, and Steve, and one of the four platforms Halden was already paying for.

"We're going to run one job through it," Marcus said. "Ridgeline. The medical offices from March. Closed, won, shipped, no harm possible. I want to watch it draft that quote the way it would have drafted it, and I want to see where it breaks."

Steve, to his credit, did not ask why now. He had the workspace open before Marcus finished the sentence, and watching him drive it was its own small education. Whatever else the eighteen months had bought, Steve's hands knew these screens. That skill was real, and it was the only thing in the room that was.

They fed it the Ridgeline request, all two hundred pages, and Marcus read Mai's rule out loud off the legal pad while Steve typed a version of it in: draft the full takeoff, price what you can verify, flag what you can't, and do not fill a gap with a guess.

The agent read two hundred pages in the time it took Marcus to get the lid off his coffee. Then it started asking where things were.

It wanted material pricing. Steve looked at Marcus. The price book was three binders, two of them at the sales desk with thirty years of pencil in the margins, one of them in purchasing and out of date since the tariff year.

It wanted machine and labor rates. Those lived in the controller's spreadsheet, which lived behind a password, which lived with the controller, who did rates on Thursdays.

It wanted supplier lead times. Marcus wrote a word on the pad and turned it toward Steve, and Steve read it and had the grace to laugh, because the word was DAN.

It wanted finish standards, and the finish standards were the finishing room, the men in it, the smell of it at six in the morning. It wanted the freight rules, and the freight rules were Sharon's corner of the whiteboard, which was not written down anywhere else on earth.

The draft came back in forty minutes, and it was, in its way, magnificent. Formatted, confident in structure, and full of holes it had refused to fill. Where the numbers should have been there were brackets, polite and relentless: NEEDED, material pricing source. NEEDED, verified lead time. ASSUMPTION NOT PERMITTED, no catalogue entry found.

Marcus had Steve print it, and counted at the table. Twenty-six brackets.

"It failed in ten minutes," Steve said, apologetic, reaching for the close button.

"Leave it up." Marcus was still reading. "It didn't fail. It's the first thing in this building that's ever told me the truth at this speed."

He had had the thought before, alone at his desk in the dark, that the machine was a hire nobody had onboarded. Now he watched the hire ask for the employee handbook, patiently, twenty-six times, and there was no handbook. There had never been a handbook. There had been Ron, and Dan, and Sharon's corner, and asking around, and twenty-two years of it working well enough that nobody had ever once written the company down.

"Question," Marcus said. "If a person needed the real lead time on rift white oak, where would they look it up?"

"They'd ask Dan."

"And the machine? Where does the machine ask?"

Steve was quiet for a moment. "Also Dan," he said. "Except it can't. That's the whole..." He stopped, and Marcus watched him arrive somewhere. "That's why the pilot died. In the spring. It wasn't the security review. It ran out of things it was allowed to know."

"Write that down," Marcus said. "That sentence. That's the first true status report this program has ever filed."

He took the printout with its twenty-six brackets and squared the pages. Bill had sat in the boardroom and asked him to point to a line, and there had been nothing to point at. This was not the number he wanted. But for the first time the program had produced a document worth pointing to: an exact map, bracket by bracket, of everything Halden Furnish knew that Halden Furnish had never written down.

"Same time Monday," Marcus said. "And Steve. Cancel the other three platforms' renewals, whatever's cancelable. We're going to be busy, and it's not going to be shopping."

He was at the door when Steve spoke.

"Nobody ever asked it to draft a real job before," Steve said. "Eighteen months. We demoed it on the vendor's samples every time." He said it the way a man reads an X-ray of his own foot. "Their samples always worked. Of course they did. The vendor wrote the floor under them."

Marcus stopped, and thought of Mai at her whiteboard, tapping the empty band. That is why the tools you bought found nothing to stand on.

"Monday," he said again, gentler, and went up to his office with twenty-six brackets under his arm.
