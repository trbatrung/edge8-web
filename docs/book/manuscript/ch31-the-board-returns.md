# Chapter 31: The board returns

Ninety days later, Marcus Vance kept the same morning. On the floor at six, a slow lap past finishing, where the lacquer crew was ahead, he could tell by smell alone. Four trucks at the dock. The fifth was in Columbus, and he knew it from Dan's board by the dock door instead of from his gut, which had checked anyway, and was still right.

He passed the sales desk last. The screens there had been on since five, overnight drafts stacked and waiting for the junior to pull apart at seven. He still looked for Ron at that desk. Ninety days had not cured it, and he had stopped expecting them to.

By five to eight he was in the boardroom on the mezzanine, the long glass wall at his back and the whole floor below it. Grace had set his place the way she had for years: the packet printed, hole-punched, tabbed in yellow. Item three was one page. Beside it, the legal pad and the fat black pen. His phone was in the truck. The screen that mattered this morning belonged to somebody else.

Cham Ridley sat in the chair against the glass, a laptop on his knees and a haircut two days old, two levels below the room and visibly aware of it. He had asked Marcus twice what to wear, and once what to do with his hands.

Walt Breen arrived first, in the navy blazer, then the two remote members on the wall monitor, then Bill Foster, exactly at eight, carrying one thin folder and no coffee. Marcus knew what the folder held, because he had posted it to the portal as item four: a resolution to close the technology line and everything under it.

Walt gaveled them through the minutes and the safety report, then cleared his throat.

"Item three. Review of the AI program, per April's resolution. Marcus, it's your floor."

"Did you read the memo Grace posted Sunday night?" Marcus asked. He was looking at Bill.

"Twice," Bill said. "It took less time than the last document I read twice."

"It's one page on purpose. Cham."

The slide went up on the monitor, the remote members shrinking to two small squares in the corner. One line of type across the top, two numbers beneath it.

Marcus took the printed copy from his packet and slid it down the table with two fingers.

"Ninety days ago you asked me to point to the line," he said. "There it is. One outcome. The complex custom quote. Eleven days then. Two now. Not the easy ones, the hard ones. Under it, two numbers. The first is what an hour of our people's time ships now, counted in finished work, not activity. The second is what moved against the outcome, including seven orders we won because we could finally quote fast enough to bid at all. That second number is not cost we saved. It is revenue that did not exist."

He stopped there. He had rehearsed stopping there, in the truck that morning, out loud, alone at a red light.

"I'm not going to talk you through the page. The person who ships those numbers keeps the ledger, and he is a better witness than I am. Cham Ridley, operations coordinator, two levels down from anyone who has ever sat at this table. Cham, you have the floor."

Cham stood, decided that was wrong, and sat back down closer to the table. His first sentence came out too fast and he started it again.

"There's a ledger. Every week since May. A unit is a piece of finished work that shipped with a person's name on it: a quote out the door, a reorder placed before the stockout, a slate of candidates screened with the reasons attached. Week one we shipped nine units and most of them were garbage. The agents invented lead times, invented finishes, because the catalogue wasn't finished, and an agent can't stand on a floor that isn't there. Week four we shipped two that were real. Last week, sixteen." He paused. "It was seventeen, but Dan Mercer bounced one back Friday. If the editor bounces it, it didn't ship."

"Whose names are these?" Bill asked. He had the printed page in front of him, one finger on the second column.

"Editors. Nothing goes out without one. The agents draft, a person signs. If it's wrong, it's wrong with a name on it, and the name is never the agent's."

"Which platform does the drafting?"

"Three different ones, and it never mattered which." He said it the way a man reports weather. "The tools were the cheap part. We're down from a hundred forty seats to forty, two platforms cancelled outright. What took the quarter was the catalogue. Six weeks pulling what this company knows out of heads and inboxes into one place an agent can read. Before that, the same tools did nothing. We watched them do nothing."

"Then who built it? The catalogue. Which vendor?"

"Nobody, ma'am. The catalogue is ours. The instructions are ours. There was nothing to buy. We looked."

"Give me the quote in hours, not days."

"A complex custom quote used to take about forty hours of estimating time spread across eleven days, most of it waiting on somebody who knew something. Now the agents draft it overnight against the catalogue, and the desk spends five or six hours editing. The judgment in the standards is Ron Hale's. We wrote it down with him before he left. One quote ran five days last month, because a supplier changed a price file mid-job and the agent flagged it instead of guessing." He said it without apology. "Ninety days ago it would have guessed. We built it to know what it doesn't know."

Bill looked at Marcus. "Your head of sales was the eleven days."

"Ron left at the end of June," Marcus said. "I owed him that conversation a year before I had it. The desk runs on a junior, the agents, and thirty years of Ron's judgment on paper. The two days cost us Ron. You won't find that on the slide, and I am not asking this room to call it a win."

Bill held his eyes a moment and let it stand. "And Marsh?"

"We ended the role. Clean terms. He told me he'd been hired to do half of a job nobody had defined, and he was right. The definition was mine to write, and for eighteen months I didn't write it."

"Headcount today?"

"One forty-two. Six we chose not to backfill, and the two roles you just asked about."

Bill went quiet. Marcus watched the arithmetic happen behind his eyes, and he said nothing, because the number was his to find.

"The seven orders," he said finally. "A hundred and ninety thousand, your memo says. Where would that revenue have been a year ago?"

"Nowhere," Cham said. "A restaurant group needed forty tables in five weeks, and we quoted it in a day and a half. We never used to lose that work. We never used to see it. Nobody waits eleven days for a price on a four-week job."

For a moment the only sound in the room was the compressor cycling somewhere beneath the floor, and Bill Foster, doing division.

"In April I asked you to name one person in this building who could do one thing a vendor didn't hand them," he said.

"You've been listening to him for twenty minutes."

Cham looked at his hands.

"And I asked which was broken. The tools or the leadership."

"The leadership," Marcus said. "Not the way either of us meant it. There was a second half to my job and nobody was doing it. I tried to buy it done. You can't. That's the whole memo."

Bill laid his hand flat on the folder. He did not open it.

"Item four is mine," he said to Walt. "I said in April that if one number in this company moved because of that money, I would withdraw the item myself." He slid the folder aside. "It's withdrawn. For the record, these are one quarter's numbers, and quarters flatter people."

"I know," Marcus said. "That's why the memo is one page."

"Then here's my motion. The line continues. Same slide, same two numbers, every quarter, and the year tells us the rest."

"Seconded," said one of the faces on the monitor.

The other face said, "Hell of a quarter, Marcus," and began, faintly, to applaud.

"Don't," Marcus said, not sharply. "It's one quarter. And I want two names in the minutes, next to the numbers. Ron Hale. Steve Marsh. The numbers were not free."

Walt looked down the table. "Anything to add, Marcus? You're owed a speech."

"No. Cham covered it."

There was a version of this morning he had drafted in June and thrown away, the one with the speech in it, the vindicated founder, the told-you-so dressed up as humility. He had thrown it away because it was a lie. You don't clap for that. You put up the slide and you sit down.

The motion carried without discussion. Walt moved them on to the bank covenants, and the meeting ran another forty minutes, and Marcus spoke perhaps ten words in all of it, because the questions kept going to Cham.

When it broke up, Bill packed his one folder and stopped at Marcus's end of the table.

"In April I asked where I could walk to touch it," he said. "The five-axis question."

"Sales desk," Marcus said. "Ask for the ledger. Cham's down there most mornings."

"And I told you not to spend the ninety days shopping."

"We spent some of them cancelling."

Something crossed his face that on another man might have been a smile. Then he was gone down the open stairs, heels on steel, out across the floor. He did not walk it like he owned a piece of it this time. He stopped at the sales desk, and Marcus stood at the glass and watched him stand there a long minute, watching the screen over the junior's shoulder, one hand flat on the desk, the way an operator touches a machine to feel whether it is running.

Marcus stayed until the room was empty. When he squared his papers, the one page sat on top. Nine drafts on the legal pad before Grace ever typed a word of it. There was no page in the packet that carried more of his pen.

On his way out he stopped at Grace's desk.

"Send a note to Mai Bennett," he said. "Tell her the item was withdrawn. She'll know what it means."

"Do you want to add anything?"

"That's the whole note," he said, and went down to the floor.
