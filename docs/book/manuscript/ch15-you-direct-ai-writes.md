# Chapter 15: You direct, AI writes

Monday he was at his desk at twenty to six, before the first cars, and the office was dark except for the screen, which was patient, which was always patient.

He propped the napkin against the lamp. He typed the seven lines in with two fingers, mistakes and all, and then, feeling like a man talking to an empty room, he typed what Priya had told him to type.

Interview me about what makes a complex custom quote good at Halden Furnish. One question at a time.

The first question was easy. The second was easy. The third one stopped him with his coffee halfway up.

You wrote, "We price the risk we're taking, not the hours we're spending." Can you give an example of a job where the hours looked fine but the risk was wrong, and who at Halden is allowed to make that call?

Fenwick. It was asking him about Fenwick, and it had never heard of Fenwick. He said the answer out loud first, alone in the dark, the whole story, the margin that looked fine and the architect nobody could pin down and the feeling Ray called weather without calling it anything. Then he typed what he had said, slower, and worse, and the machine asked the next question.

If "the feeling" is load-bearing in that decision, what facts is the feeling usually made of? List them even if they seem obvious.

"They're not obvious," Marcus said to the empty office, and then spent forty minutes proving it to himself, because the facts came slowly, one at a time, like bolts backing out of old wood, and every one of them was something he had known for two decades and never once said. Architects who redline twice. Boards that fight after the deposit clears. The customer who asks about the warranty before the price, and what that means, and what you do about it.

At some point the floor came alive below him and he did not notice. At some point Grace put coffee at his elbow, the second one, and he did not hear her do it. When he finally sat back the clock said ten past nine, and the morning was gone, and it had not felt like a morning going. It had felt the way afternoons felt in the rented-bay years, when the company was one truck and his own two hands, and the hours went by without asking permission.

On the screen was a document. HALDEN FURNISH: WHAT A GOOD QUOTE IS. Two pages. Every sentence in it was his, said out loud in the dark to a patient machine, and the machine had done the thing he had never had words to delegate: it had taken the say and made it stay. Organized, ordered, each judgment with its example, each rule with its reason, and nothing in it invented, because he had told it the rule about inventing and it had kept the rule.

He read it twice. Then he typed, hunt and peck, one more instruction.

Now write the instructions for an agent that drafts quotes to this standard. It must verify every fact against our catalogue. It must flag anything it cannot verify. It must never fill a gap with a guess.

The instructions came back, and he read every line, braced for the moment it would leave him behind, the syntax, the technical part, the part that was for other people.

The moment never came. He understood every line, because every line was his. Somewhere in the middle he caught himself reaching for the pen, marking a sentence, sharpening it the way he sharpened contract language, writing NO in a margin that wasn't paper, and he realized what he was doing. He was directing. Something that never got tired was writing. That was the whole of it. That was the entire mystery he had paid four hundred thousand dollars to keep at arm's length.

The humbling part arrived quietly, after, with the second cold coffee.

No bootcamp could have taught him this morning. Not because the bootcamp was bad, but because the curriculum had been locked in his own head, unwritten, the whole time. The certificate on the heavy stock said he had attended FOUNDATIONS OF AI, and the foundation had turned out to be Fenwick, and weather, and what a promise was allowed to stand on, and no one on earth could have handed him that in a hotel ballroom, because it wasn't theirs to hand.

You could buy the shelves. You could not buy the books. He got out the legal pad and wrote that down under the nine names, because it was the closest he had come yet to the sentence he owed Ellery Kwan in July.

He printed both documents, the standard and the instructions, and carried them out to Grace's desk warm.

"File these," he said. "Master copy."

Grace read the top page, then looked up at him over the readers she denied wearing.

"Who wrote this?"

"I did," Marcus said, and heard how strange it sounded, and how true. He was fifty-four years old, and it was the first document with his name behind it in years that he had not merely signed.

"Under what?" Grace asked, pen ready. "Where does it file?"

He thought about the banker's box downstairs, the drawer upstairs, the card, the attendance certificates on heavy stock, eighteen months of paper that ended in paper.

"Start a new drawer," he said.
