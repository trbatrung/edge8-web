# Chapter 1: The number that isn't there

Marcus Vance had kept the same morning for twenty-two years. On the floor at six, a slow lap past finishing, where he could tell by smell alone whether the lacquer crew was ahead or behind. Four trucks at the dock. He knew without looking that the fifth was stuck somewhere south of Dayton.

By five to eight he was in the boardroom on the mezzanine, the long glass wall at his back and the whole floor below it. Grace had set his place the way she had for years: the board packet printed, hole-punched, tabbed in yellow where she thought he would want to speak. Beside it, his legal pad and the fat black pen he had owned longer than the company. The other places were bare. The board carried their own screens. His phone was in the truck. In twenty-two years he had never brought a screen into that room, and he was quietly proud of never needing one.

Walt Breen arrived first, in the navy blazer he had worn to every meeting since the credit facility in 2011, then the two remote members on the wall monitor, then Ellery Kwan, exactly at eight, carrying one thin folder and no coffee.

Walt gaveled them through the minutes and the safety report, then cleared his throat.

"Item three. Review of the AI program. Ellery asked for it, so Ellery has the floor."

"Did you read the memo I posted to the portal Sunday night?" Ellery asked. She was looking at Marcus.

"Grace prints my materials Monday mornings."

"Then you've had it for two hours. Good enough." She opened the folder. "Eighteen months ago this board approved a technology line without a ceiling, because Marcus asked us to trust him. I went back through all of it. Trevor Nash, Director of AI Innovation, two hundred thousand a year, thirteen months on payroll. Licenses, one hundred forty seats across four platforms, fifty-two thousand a year, eighteen months of it. The bootcamp last spring, every employee, two days, thirty-eight thousand, plus two days of a stopped floor, call that twelve more. The Corrigan Group engagement, sixty-two thousand, concluded in March. All in, a little over four hundred thousand dollars."

"Those numbers are right," Marcus said.

"I know they're right. Here's my question." She took a single page from the folder and slid it down the table. "That's the P&L, same eighteen months. Show me where the four hundred thousand comes back. Point to the line."

Marcus stopped the page with two fingers and did not look down. "It doesn't work like that. Not in the first..."

"Point to the line."

"You don't judge a foundation by the first rain that hits it."

"I'm not judging a foundation. I'm looking for one. Margin, flat. Revenue per employee, down a point. Overtime in the shop, up. Show me one number in this company that moved because of that money and I will withdraw the item myself."

Marcus had three answers ready. He had rehearsed them in the truck that morning, out loud, alone at a red light, the way he rehearsed everything that mattered.

He gave the first one slowly, in the voice he used with banks. "In 2009 this board, some of you were on it, told me the five-axis machine was a quarter million dollars of vanity. Two years later it carried the Meridian contract, and Meridian carried us through the recession. Some investments you make before you can prove them. That is what founders are for."

"I love that story," Ellery said. "Here's the difference. I can walk downstairs right now and put my hand on the five-axis. I can stand there and watch it cut. Where do I walk to touch this? What do I watch it do?"

"Trevor gave this board a demonstration in January," Marcus said. "You clapped, Walt."

"It was a nice demonstration," Walt said mildly.

"It was a demo of a vendor's product running a vendor's example," Ellery said.

The second answer came out faster than he meant it to. "Ask anyone what a manufacturer our size should be doing about AI. The association, the trade press, the bank's own quarterly letter. They will hand you our exact shopping list. A director. Tools. Training. Outside expertise. We did it early and we did it right."

"When the Corrigan people finished in March, what stayed behind?"

"Their recommendations. The roadmap."

"A deck. Forty-one slides. I read it twice. The last one says, next steps: continue the journey."

Nobody laughed.

"So name one person in this building who can do one thing today that a vendor didn't hand them," she said. "One person. One thing."

Marcus opened his mouth for the third answer and found that it was not there.

That had never happened to him in this room. He reached for the floor instead, the way he always did, and pictured it: purchasing on the phone with the same suppliers, working the same three spreadsheets. Shipping running off the whiteboard by the dock door. Until this morning he would have called that stability. He could not make it answer her question.

"The bootcamp trained every employee we have," he said.

"It seated every employee you have. Nobody had to prove a thing at the end of it. Seated and trained are different words and you know it." She turned a page without hurry. "Operational check, then. Your average quote on a complex custom job. Eighteen months ago, what was it?"

"Eleven days."

"Today?"

Marcus's eyes went briefly to the glass, toward the sales desk, and came back.

"Eleven days."

Ellery nodded slowly, as if he had confirmed something she had hoped was wrong, and slid a second page down after the first.

"License usage. Trevor's office sent it over the same day I asked, credit to him. One hundred forty seats. In the last ninety days, nine were opened at all. I sorted it by last login." She paused. "I'll spare the room the bottom of the list."

Marcus did not turn it over. He laid it face down on the first page and squared the edges with his thumb. Somewhere in his desk drawer was the card Trevor had printed for him his first week, a username and a temporary password. The password, he supposed, was temporary still.

"Say what you came to say, Ellery."

She closed the folder.

"We bought the tools everyone said to buy. Either the tools are broken or the leadership is. Which is it?"

For a moment the only sound in the room was the compressor cycling somewhere beneath the floor.

"The leadership," Marcus said, "took a rented bay and one truck and built the most trusted custom shop in three states. The leadership has made payroll five hundred and some times in a row. Ask anyone on that floor whether the leadership is broken. I have never pretended to be a technology man. I have never needed to be one. When the job called for one, I went out and hired one, the same way I hired a controller and a safety officer, and I put real money behind him, because half measures are how companies our size die. If it needs more time, that is because we are doing it right instead of doing it loud."

It was a good speech. He heard it land the way good speeches land, respectfully, and change nothing.

"I've given that speech," Ellery said, and there was no cruelty in it, which made it worse. "Marcus, I ran a plant for twelve years. I've signed the front of more paychecks than anyone else at this table, and I have signed off on my own share of spend that went nowhere, so hear me: this is not fashion and it is not personal. Four hundred thousand dollars left this company and nothing came back. Not a dollar, not an hour, not a skill anyone kept. If one of your plant managers spent that with nothing to show, he would be sitting in this room explaining himself. Somebody has to put you in the room. That is the job."

Walt raised a hand before Marcus could answer.

"Proposal," Walt said. "Because we are not settling 'which is it' this morning. Marcus, take the quarter. One fiscal quarter. Come back and show us where the four hundred thousand lives in this business. A number, in the P&L or visibly on its way to it. If you can't, we kill the line, all of it, and we deal with whatever that means."

"Whatever that means," Ellery said, quietly.

Nobody said the other thing, the thing about founders and boards and what happens when a number does not come back. Nobody had to.

"Seconded," said one of the faces on the monitor.

Walt looked down the table. "I'd rather have your yes, Marcus."

Marcus looked at the two pages, the one with the number that was there and the one with the number that wasn't. Then out the glass at the floor, first shift fully on now, every station running exactly the way he had built it to run.

"One quarter is more than I need," he said.

He heard it come out in the voice he used with customers, the confident one, the closing one, and knew it for what it was. A bid on a job he had not scoped.

The motion carried without discussion. Walt moved them on to the bank covenants, and the meeting ran another forty minutes, and Marcus spoke perhaps ten words in all of it.

When it broke up, Ellery packed her one folder and stopped at his end of the table.

"Ninety days," she said. "And a word of advice you didn't ask for. Try not to spend them shopping."

Then she was gone down the open stairs, heels on steel, out across his floor, walking it like she owned a piece of it, which she did.

Marcus stayed until the room was empty. When he squared his papers, the packet fell open to the appendix, the AI program summary, fourteen pages Trevor had written in a font that did not match the rest. Every other page carried his pen. Circles, question marks, arrows running into the margins. These fourteen pages were clean. He had read them the way a man reads a menu in a language he does not speak. Politely, trusting somebody else to order.

He gathered his packet and legal pad. The license report he left where it lay, face down on the polished table.

On his way out he stopped at Grace's desk.

"Pull me everything we bought under the AI line," he said. "Every license, every invoice, every login. All of it, one place."

"Do you want me to send it to you?"

"Print it," he said, and went down to the floor.
