# Chapter 16: The zeal, and the warning

The week ran hot.

He was at his desk in the dark every morning, the machine patient, the pad filling. Tuesday they wrote down what a good reorder was. Wednesday he spent an hour at the whiteboard by the dock door with Sharon, asking her what the marks in her corner meant, and she told him, suspicious the whole time that he was up to something, which he was. By Thursday, Sharon's corner existed on paper for the first time in eleven years, and Sharon had read the printout with her arms crossed and made four corrections and initialed it, and the initialing, Marcus suspected, had quietly meant something to both of them. By Friday the machine had written a small checker off the initialed rules, a program that read every outgoing confirmation against Sharon's corner before it went anywhere, and Sharon, who trusted nothing on a screen, spent her lunch trying to fool it four ways and managed it once, and the once went back into the rules the same afternoon.

He felt fifteen years younger and slightly feverish. He recognized the condition. It was how he had felt the year they poured the second building's slab.

Friday night he stayed late with the conference room whiteboard, and that was where it got away from him.

It started innocently, sorting. He had standards accumulating, agents to point at things, nine names becoming pages in a catalogue, and a founder's itch to make it all line up. So he wiped the board and drew four big boxes, and grouped his whole company into them, and stood back, and felt the old feeling, the dangerous one, the one that came right before he was sure.

Everything that sold went in one box. Everything that ran went in another. Everything that hired and kept people went in a third. And the fourth box he stood in front of for a while and finally labeled NEXT, for the things nobody at Halden ever had time to build, and it looked so clean up there, the whole company in four rooms, that by nine o'clock he was drawing the new reporting lines. Dan over all of RUN, formally this time. The sales desk relocated into SELL with an agent-half drawn wide beside it, Mai's trick, boxes with room in them. New titles. Moved desks. He filled the board, stepped back, and admired the first reorganization he had drawn in a decade, and it admired him back.

He called Mai Saturday morning from his kitchen, too pleased to wait, and read it to her. The four boxes. The new lines. The titles. He was most of the way through SELL when she said it.

"Stop."

"You haven't heard the..."

"Marcus. Don't move a single desk yet."

The kitchen clock ticked, wrong as always.

"It's a good structure," he said, and heard the boardroom creep into his voice, the defending register, and hated it a little.

"It might be. Someday. That's not the point." She exhaled, and when she spoke again it was slower, the voice from the whiteboard, the diagnosis voice. "You've had one good week. One. And you spent Friday night doing the thing you were already the best at twenty years ago. Drawing boxes is the old half of your job wearing the new half's clothes. Boxes feel like progress because they obey you instantly. The floor doesn't obey you. The floor is nine names and a thousand facts and every one of them has to be dragged into daylight one at a time, and none of that gets easier because a man moved the desks over the weekend."

"The structure would drive the..."

"Who's in the fourth box?"

Marcus stopped.

"The one you labeled NEXT," Mai said. "The things nobody has time to build. Name the people in it. Right now, off your chart."

He looked at the sunlit kitchen table where the legal pad lay, and he did not need to look. He knew what was in the fourth box. Arrows pointed into it. Nothing lived there.

"It's empty."

"It's empty. And I will tell you something for free, because it cost me two years. It will still be empty after you move every desk in that building, because reorganizing doesn't make time, it spends it. Every hour your people burn learning new reporting lines is an hour that box doesn't get. Pour the floor, Marcus. Write the company down. Point the agents you have at the outcomes you can name, and let the boxes tell you later where they want to be. The chart is the last thing you draw, not the first."

He stood at the window. Outside, the barn he had once made his daughter back a trailer into needed paint on the north side, the way it had needed it for three years, because there was never time, and he heard the fourth box laugh at him, quietly, from two towns away.

"You're right," he said, and was surprised to find that only most of him meant it.

"I know I'm right. I'm the world's leading expert in this exact mistake." A beat, and then, drier: "You did good work this week. The Sharon thing. That was the real job. Do forty more of those."

They hung up. Marcus stood in the kitchen a while, feeling the fever cool into something more useful, and made his peace with Monday's version of the plan, the boring one, the floor.

The sketch was still on the conference room whiteboard. He'd erase it Monday, first thing, before the seven-thirty production meeting.

It was only a sketch. Nobody else was going to see it.
