# Chapter 4: The private admission

Jocelyn came out on Sunday, the way she did once a month or so, with bread from the good bakery by her apartment and a bottle of wine she knew he would not open. Marcus made the pork chops he always made. It was the one meal he had ever mastered and they both pretended that was a joke.

She ran programs for a hospital foundation in the city, and she talked about it while he cooked. Somewhere in it she mentioned, in passing, that she had spent the drive out dictating donor letters to something on her phone, and that it had drafted all forty by the time she parked, in her voice, and that she had fixed three of them and sent the rest. She said it the way she would have said she stopped for gas. He stood at the stove with his back to her and heard the whole four hundred thousand dollars go by in one sentence.

Over dinner she asked about the board meeting, because Grace talked to her more than either of them admitted, and he gave her the performance. Ellery's folder. The one-quarter ultimatum, delivered the way he delivered it at the club, timed, with the pauses in it. He was good at the story by now. That was the problem with him and stories.

Jocelyn did not laugh in any of the places the men at the club had laughed.

"You're doing the jaw thing," she said.

"There's no jaw thing."

"There's been a jaw thing since I was nine. What did she actually say?"

He looked at his plate. Then, because it was Jocelyn, and because the kitchen was the one room left where he had never once been a chief executive, he told her the true version. The line that wasn't there. The question about the tools and the leadership. The license report he had laid face down, and the login card in his desk drawer, fourteen months old, with the temporary password that had told the truth about him all along.

"You know what I keep thinking about," she said when he finished. "The trailer."

"What trailer?"

"Dad. The trailer. I was fifteen. You made me back it into the barn until it was dark out. No mirrors folded in, no getting out to look, do it again, do it again." She was half smiling. "I cried in that truck."

"You were terrible at it."

"I was fifteen. That was the point, you said. You said, learn it now, because nobody wants to teach you when you're fifty."

She heard it land and put her fork down.

"Dad. I didn't mean it like that."

"I know what you meant." He set his knife across the plate, squared it without thinking. "I meant it, though. Whoever I was when I said it."

The kitchen was quiet except for the clock his wife's mother had given them thirty years ago, which had never once kept good time and had never once been taken down.

"I'm afraid I missed the turn," Marcus said.

It came out plainly, at last, without the club timing, and it surprised him, how small it sounded, for something he had been carrying around like a chest freezer.

"There's a phrase they use now. I read it in one of the magazines on some airplane. A legacy CEO." He turned his water glass a quarter turn. "It means the company would be modern by now if the founder would just die a little faster."

Jocelyn didn't laugh, which he appreciated, because it had not entirely been a joke.

"Fifty-four is not old," she said.

"No. But I've been running that place out of my own head since you were in grade school, and every year it got easier to believe that was a skill instead of a habit. The board sat there Monday and asked me what I'd built with all that money, and Joss, the honest answer is I bought things. I bought things so I wouldn't have to learn things. Nobody made me do it in the dark until I could do it. There's nobody above me left to make me."

She let that sit the way her mother used to let things sit.

"Can I say something you'll hate?"

"You'll say it anyway."

"You're not afraid of the technology, Dad. You've never been afraid of a machine in your life. You bought a machine the size of a garage in the middle of a recession." She leaned back. "You're afraid of being a beginner. You haven't been one in forty years, and you'd rather be a fool with a checkbook than a beginner in front of your own people."

Marcus looked at his daughter for a long moment.

"That's about the size of it," he said.

They did the dishes the way they always did, him washing, her drying, the radio low. She talked about other things, kindly, and he let her, and was grateful. At the door she hugged him hard and told him the pork chops were better than usual, which was a lie with forty years of love in it, and drove off with her headlights sweeping the barn he had once made her back a trailer into.

Marcus stood in the kitchen a while after the sound of her car was gone.

Fifty-four. Somewhere in the last five years, without ever deciding it, he had become a man who did not start things he might be bad at. He dried his hands and hung the towel on the oven door, square, the way it went.

Saying the fear out loud had not made it smaller. It had only made it his.
