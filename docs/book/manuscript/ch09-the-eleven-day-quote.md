# Chapter 9: The eleven-day quote

Priya gave him an hour the following Thursday, and named her price on the phone, which was that he bring the chart.

"The one on your pad. Don't redraw it pretty. I want the one with the empty boxes."

Her office was the way it had been, the whiteboard wiped clean, and she started with a question instead of a marker.

"Walk me through a quote at Halden. A big one, custom, the kind you're known for. Door to door."

"It comes in," Marcus said. "Usually to Ray directly, the customers ask for him. He works it. It goes back out."

"That's three steps and one of them is a man's name. Walk me through the middle."

Marcus opened his mouth to walk her through the middle and found he was standing at the edge of it. There were drawings, he knew that. There were calls to suppliers. There was a period, somewhere in the second week, when the folder came upstairs for his signature and went back down. He had watched Ray work for two hours on Friday and could describe the weather of it, the pencil, the ten-key, the calls about knee surgery that were really about schedules. He could not have drawn it as steps to save his life.

"It goes in a door," he said finally, "and comes out eleven days later, right."

"Right." No triumph in it, as usual. "Now watch mine."

She uncapped the black marker and drew an org chart, and Marcus understood within three boxes that this was not the company chart. It was one job, the quote, drawn as if the quote were a team.

The first box read INTAKE, and she drew it with a double line. "Agent. It reads the request the night it lands. Every drawing, every spec, every line of the email chain. It files what it finds against the catalogue and writes up what the job actually is, which is not always what the customer says it is."

Double line again. TAKEOFF. "Agent. Overnight it drafts the whole build: materials, hours, lead times, freight. Everything it can verify against the catalogue, it prices. Everything it can't, it flags. It is not allowed to fill a gap with a guess. That rule cost us more sweat than any other rule in the building."

Then a box with a single line and a name in it. TERESA. "Person. My estimator. She comes in at seven to a drafted quote and a list of flags, and her whole day is judgment. She rules on the flags. She reads the customer. She decides where we want the job and where we don't. Her name goes on the number, because a quote is a promise, and promises need a person."

One more single-lined box, small, at the end. "Second pair of eyes on anything over fifty thousand. Me, usually, for ten minutes."

She capped the marker.

"Four boxes. Two of the who are agents. The thing you watched trickle through your shop for eleven days, this team does before the afternoon truck leaves."

Marcus looked at the chart and did the disloyal arithmetic. The Caldwell courthouse was at day eight, back at his shop, right now.

There was a knock, and a woman leaned in without fully entering, thirty-something, reading glasses pushed up in her hair. "The rail package from this morning's out. He wants it broken into two POs, I let it, the flags were all seconder stuff." She registered Marcus, nodded politely, and was gone.

"That came in at six forty," Priya said, "while you were driving here."

Marcus took out his watch the way other men checked phones. It was five past two.

He looked back at the whiteboard, at the single-lined box with TERESA in it, the person whose day was judgment, whose name went on the number. And before he could stop himself he had put a face in the box, a particular face, sixty-three years old, glasses folded on a binder, and the face did not fit in the box and the box did not care, and Marcus looked away from the whiteboard the way a man steps over a gap in a dock.

Priya watched him do it, and let it alone, the way she had let the fence alone.

"Questions," she said.

"A hundred. Give me a minute to lose the dumb ones." He rolled the pad's pages back and forth. "The overnight part. The drafting. Where does it get the prices, the lead times, all of it?"

"The catalogue. The floor under the chart, the band you couldn't fill in. Two years of writing down what was true. Every price, every supplier's real lead time, not the printed one, the real one. Every rule about what good looks like. The agents stand on that or they stand on nothing. You already know what they do when they stand on nothing."

"They find nothing."

"Worse," Priya said. "Sometimes they find something anyway."

She let that sit exactly one beat, and moved on, and he would remember it later, the way you remember a road sign after the ditch.

He stayed the full hour. She never named a product, and he never asked. At the end she handed him nothing, no printout, no photograph, no follow-up in his inbox by the time he reached the lot. She simply stood and waited while he copied the four boxes onto his legal pad, on the page after his own company, the chart with the empty halves and then the team of one job, facing each other through the paper.

On the drive home he did not think about Ray.

He did not think about Ray through the roadwork on the county line, and did not think about him past the fairgrounds, and was still not thinking about him at the long red at Route 9, which is a way of thinking about a man, and he knew it.

Day eight, the Caldwell quote. It would go out Tuesday, and it would be right, because it always was.

For the first time in twenty-two years, he heard the eleven days the way Ellery Kwan would have heard them. He heard what they cost.
