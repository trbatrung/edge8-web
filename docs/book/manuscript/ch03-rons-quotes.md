# Chapter 3: Ray's quotes

The audit took the rest of the week. Marcus did it on foot, the way he had done inventory in the early years, Grace half a step behind him with the folder. Purchasing turned out to run on three spreadsheets and a telephone. Scheduling ran on the whiteboard by the dock door, and one corner of the whiteboard belonged to Sharon, and nobody wrote in Sharon's corner, and nobody could say what happened if Sharon was out, because Sharon was never out. HR was two people bailing a boat. Each of them got a page on the legal pad, and by Thursday the pad was heavy with his pen.

Friday morning he came to the sales desk, and the audit stopped being an audit.

Ray Delgado had the Caldwell County courthouse spread across the whole desk, drawings weighted flat with two sample blocks, walnut and white oak. Day three of the quote. Thirty-eight benches, a judge's dais, panelling for two courtrooms, and a jury box the county wanted to look exactly like the one it was replacing, except that a man could sit down in it without the whole room hearing him do it.

Marcus took the leather customer chair, which on any ordinary morning was the best seat in the building, and watched.

It was like watching a man play an instrument he had built himself. Ray worked with a pencil and the old ten-key, and mostly he worked out loud, not to Marcus, to the job. "Freight elevator's seven foot even," he said, to the dais drawing. "So you go up in pieces, don't you." He knew that courthouse's elevator the way Marcus knew his own dock, without looking at anything. The desk pad under his forearms had gone gray with graphite, phone numbers written over phone numbers, thirty years deep.

At nine he called the county's facilities man, and the call ran twenty minutes, of which maybe four were about the courthouse. The rest was the man's knee, and a fishing cabin, and the correct way to season a smoker, and by the end of it Ray knew, without once asking, that the funding vote was already safe, that the administrator wanted the benches delivered first so the commissioners would have something to sit on and stop calling him, and that the only thing anybody over there was truly afraid of was the schedule.

"Benches first," Ray said to Casey when he hung up. Casey was the younger of his two inside reps. "Write that down somewhere you'll believe it later."

Casey wrote it down, on the back of a printout, which went onto a stack of printouts. Casey wrote everything down, and watching him keep it was like watching a man carry water in his hands.

Mid-morning Walter Brandt came by in person, which he did three or four times a year, almost always when he needed nothing. Brandt put up medical buildings, had for twenty years, and every one of them had a lobby with Halden in it.

"Ray Delgado," Brandt said. "Still here. They tell me you've got a machine now that does your job."

"They tell me the same about you," Ray said, and neither of them bothered to laugh, and it was the warmest sound on the floor all morning.

Brandt stayed twenty minutes. He was breaking ground in the spring, out by the interstate, his biggest one yet. "Don't let anybody else touch the lobby," he said on his way out, to Ray, not to Marcus, and Ray said, "Have I ever," and Brandt raised a hand without turning around, which among men like Brandt was an embrace.

Marcus watched all of it with something that had no good business name. Pride was close. The desk was the one part of Halden that had never once made him nervous. Banks made him rehearse. Boards made him rehearse. Ray made him feel the way the smell of the finishing room made him feel at six in the morning, that the world had at least one corner where things were exactly what he had built them to be.

There had been a moment, a year or so back, when Trevor had floated something at a staff meeting, a takeoff tool, quoting software, something with a dashboard in it. Marcus had ended the conversation with one sentence. He could no longer remember the sentence. He could remember the feeling, which was the feeling of a man closing a gate, and he had never once revisited it, the way you never revisit a thing you are sure of.

The Caldwell number would go out a week from Monday. Eleven days, near enough. It was always near enough eleven days, and in twenty-two years Marcus had never heard the number as a cost. It was the sound of the thing being done right. You did not frame a courthouse in a weekend, and you did not price one in an afternoon.

At the end of the morning Ray looked up from the drawings for the first time.

"You've been in that chair two hours," he said. "Either I'm getting audited or you're hiding from something upstairs."

"Can't a man watch?"

"A man can watch." Ray turned the drawing around, tapped the jury box. "Look at that. County's got a fellow who can draw a creak. Right there, that rail. That's where it'll sing if the shop gets proud with the joinery." He turned it back. "Go on upstairs, Marcus. Nothing down here needs fixing."

Marcus went up the open stairs. From the mezzanine glass the sales desk was a lamp and a bent head and a boy carrying water in his hands, and it looked, as it had looked for thirty years, like the safest thing he owned.

That afternoon Grace typed up the audit. Every function in the building had a page. Purchasing, scheduling, shipping, HR, finance, the floor.

There was no page for the sales desk. It had not occurred to him to start one.
