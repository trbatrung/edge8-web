# Chapter 14: I don't do the technical part

They met Saturday morning at a diner off the county road, roughly halfway between their shops, the kind of place with egg plates and a pie case and nothing on the wall newer than either of them. Priya was there first, in a booth by the window, reading actual paper, which he had somehow known she would be.

Marcus slid in across from her and laid it all out with the coffee: the nine names clipped to the pad, the twenty-six brackets, and the plan. He was proud of the plan. It had structure.

"Trevor owns the catalogue build. I bring in two engineers from the license vendor, they've offered before, to structure the data properly. We stand up a steering committee, I chair it, monthly. Dana and Ray feed their knowledge in through working sessions. Six months, we have a floor under the company."

Priya read the page. Then she squared it on the table and slid it back, and he recognized the gesture with a small interior flinch, because it was his own, the license report, face down, edges even.

"Who decides what a good quote looks like at Halden?"

"Ray. Me."

"Who decides what margin is fair on a one-off? Which customers get walked through the number and which get one page? What a promise is allowed to be built on?"

"Me. That's not..."

"Then tell me exactly what the vendor's engineers are going to write down." She let it sit. "They can build shelves, Marcus. Good shelves, fast. But every book on those shelves is a judgment, and the judgments are yours and Ray's and Dana's, and the vendor's men have never once stood in a customer's unfinished lobby. You're about to do it again. Same move, nicer suit. You're going to sponsor the work instead of doing it, and chair the committee that watches other people not know what only you know."

"I'm not going to type the company into a database myself."

"Nobody types anymore. That's not the part I'm worried about." She stirred her coffee once. "Say the real objection. The one under that one."

Marcus looked out the window at the trucks.

"I have never pretended to be a technical man," he said, and heard it leave his mouth in the exact voice he had used in the boardroom in April, the proud voice, the one that had made it sound like a credential.

"There it is." Priya nodded slowly. "You hold that sentence up like a doctor's note. Twenty-two years. Excused from gym."

"It's a fact."

"It was a fact about typing. It was never a fact about you." She leaned forward. "You've written instructions your whole life. You wrote them for crews, for banks, for your daughter backing a trailer, I'd bet. And you wrote one set badly and paid four hundred thousand dollars for it. You wrote Trevor's job description. Evaluate platforms. Manage the rollout. You wrote the wrong instructions, and a good man executed them faithfully, and you got exactly what you wrote. Don't tell me you can't write instructions, Marcus. Tell me you're ready to write the right ones."

The eggs came. Marcus didn't touch his for a while.

"Here." Priya took the pen out of his shirt pocket, the fat black one, and put it on a napkin, and pushed both across. "Ten lines. What makes a quote good at Halden. Plain English, like you're leaving instructions for a smart new hire before a two-week vacation. Go."

"Now?"

"You have somewhere to be?"

He wrote A good quote is accurate, and crossed it out, because it was a poster, not an instruction. He wrote Nothing goes out with an unverified lead time and left it. He wrote Margin reflects and sat there, and thought about the Fenwick job they had passed on when the margin looked fine, and why, and how he would explain the why to a smart stranger, and it took him three tries to get one sentence that held: We price the risk we're taking, not the hours we're spending, and if we can't name the risk we don't sign the number.

It took twenty minutes to get seven lines, and three of them were crossed out, and his eggs went cold, and at no point in any of it had there been anything a person could call typing.

He put the pen down. "That's hard."

"Hardest work in the building." Priya was mopping her plate. "Now tell me which part of it was technical."

He looked at the napkin. The hard part had been knowing what he meant. The hard part had been Fenwick, and the risk, and twenty-two years of pricing decisions he had never once had to say out loud because they lived where nobody could check them. The hard part was judgment, dragged into daylight, made to explain itself.

"None of it," he said.

"None of it. And no engineer, no bootcamp, and no forty-one-slide deck could have written a word of it, because the material only exists in one place." She pointed the fork at him, briefly, and put it down.

"Three skills, Marcus. That's the whole second half, sitting under everything you watched at my shop. Deciding who does what, when some of the who are agents. Workflow design. You started that one in my parking lot, fourteen boxes, eleven of them waiting. Organizing what's true so a person or an agent can find it in seconds. Information architecture. Your nine names are page one of that. And this one." She tapped the napkin. "Writing instructions for machines. And I mean past prompts. The bootcamp did prompts, didn't it. A day on magic words."

"I read something like this once," Marcus said slowly. "Some piece, no name on it, middle of the night. It used almost these words."

"I'd bet you did. There's no shortage of pieces." Priya was unimpressed in a way that felt, oddly, like respect. "Read one of those at midnight and you get three words. Workflow design. Information architecture. Instructions. You've had the words since that night, and you still couldn't draw your own quote as boxes until you sat forty minutes in my parking lot. The word was never the missing part, Marcus. It never is. That's the whole reason a bootcamp can hand out a hundred and fifty pieces of paper and Halden still couldn't answer one question from its own board."

"A day and a half."

"A prompt is a favor. You ask nicely and you hope. An instruction is a standard with consequences: workflows, rules, code when it needs to be code. You don't type the code, you direct and the machine writes it, but there is no directing off a cheat sheet." She slid the napkin back across. "Tools come and go. The day the vendors change everything, the three skills are what you still own. Which is why nobody could ever sell them to you."

"Monday morning," she said, "before the floor wakes up. You, that machine you already pay for, and the napkin. Ask it to interview you about the rest. One question at a time. That's the whole assignment. You'll know by nine o'clock whether I'm wrong."

She picked up the check over his objection, which he understood by now was not politeness but policy, and at the register she said the only encouraging thing she would say all day.

"Seven lines, first sitting, eggs going cold." She counted out singles for the tip. "Took me a month to get seven I'd keep."

Marcus drove home with a napkin in his shirt pocket, ten lines, three of them crossed out, and it weighed more than the whole banker's box.
