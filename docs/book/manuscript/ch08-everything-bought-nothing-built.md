# Chapter 8: Everything bought, nothing built

The drive home from Priya's was forty minutes, and Marcus drove it slowly, radio off, the legal pad on the seat beside him with the chart face up. Twenty-two years of his decisions in black pen, and the two kinds of empty.

He thought about Trevor most of the way.

For eighteen months he had been grading Trevor the way you grade a machine that will not run right, patiently, then less patiently, waiting for the output. Somewhere around the county line the grade changed. Trevor had done, faithfully and on schedule, every line of a job description Marcus had signed without reading. Evaluate platforms. He had evaluated platforms; there were four of them. Build the vendor relationships. The vendors loved him. Manage the rollout, run enablement, and the rollout had been managed and the enablement run, and a hundred and fifty certificates said so. The one thing the paper had never asked him to do was build anything into anybody who worked at Halden, and so, in eighteen months, working hard the whole time, he never had.

You could not call a man incompetent for finishing the wrong list. Mis-hired, maybe. Mis-briefed, certainly. And the brief was Marcus's.

Then he thought about the five-axis, because he always thought about the five-axis, except that this time he made himself remember all of it.

The story he told boards ended with the Meridian contract. The story started somewhere else. It started with Tomas and Dale Pruitt on a plane to Cincinnati, five weeks at the manufacturer's school, full wages the whole time, while the machine was still being crated in Germany. It started with the two of them coming home and training two more. By the time the five-axis landed on his floor it landed in the hands of men who could already run it, and in all the years of telling that story, Marcus had never once thought of the school as separate from the machine. The school was simply what buying it meant. You did not bolt half a million dollars to your floor and then go looking for someone to rent.

Eighteen months ago he had bought four platforms and sent nobody anywhere.

He went down the AI line the way he had gone through Grace's box, and this time he could read it. The licenses were capability, rented by the month, and they sat in a drawer because a rented capability with nobody trained under it is a drawer, whatever the invoice calls it. The bootcamp was knowledge, two days of it, and knowledge without practice had gone home as card stock. And Corrigan had been judgment, somebody else's judgment, hired by the pound, and it drove away in March with everything Halden had paid it to think.

Bought, bought, bought. Nothing built. Not because anyone had failed him. Because building was the one line that had never appeared on any invoice, and he was the only man in the company who could have written it in.

At Priya's, the morning meeting ran off a sheet her own scheduler had built, and when the woman retired, the sheet would stay. The screen over the press brake had cost a winter of one operator's evenings, and it could not be canceled by a vendor, because there was no vendor, there was only a man who now knew how. Ellery had stood in his boardroom and asked him to name one person who could do one thing a vendor didn't hand them, and he had wanted, badly, to answer her. Three weeks later he finally could. At Priya's shop: all of them, down to the scheduler. At his: not one.

Including him. He made himself say that part. Especially him.

Route 9 took him past his own building. Nine o'clock, and three windows were lit, the way there were always windows lit, somebody catching up, and for twenty-two years the sight of his company working late had warmed him like a stove. He slowed, and looked, and drove on, and it did not warm him. Somewhere behind one of those windows, somebody was asking a question that only one other person in the building could answer, and that person had gone home, and so the somebody would wait. He had built that. He had called it a family.

The long red at the Route 9 junction caught him a mile later, the same light where he had rehearsed the three answers, the same light where he had counted his week and come up empty. He sat through it with his hands loose on the wheel and nothing rehearsed at all. No defenses. No speech. Nothing in his mouth, and for the first time in a month, the nothing did not feel like losing. It felt like a cleared bench at the start of a job.

The tools hadn't failed. He could say it now without flinching. The tools were never the thing. The job had changed, quietly, some year while he was busy being good at the half of it he knew, and the half that had arrived could not be bought, not with a director, not with a bootcamp, not with a deck, because what it was made of was his own judgment, standing somewhere it had never stood.

The light changed. He drove home, and set the legal pad on the kitchen table where he would see it at breakfast, squared to the edge, the chart face up, the empty band waiting under the line.

Priya had told him to look at it in the morning and ask what went there. Not which tool. What was true, and where it lived, and who was going to write it down.

He was fifty-four years old, and tomorrow, in front of people, he was going to be a beginner.
